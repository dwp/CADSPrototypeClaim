Applying for Carer's Allowance

This guide gives general information only and is not a complete statement of the law.

On this page:

  2. The person you care for gets other benefits
  3. Your partner

1. Backdating your claim date

Most claims for Carer's Allowance can be backdated 3 months. You should claim from the date you began caring for the person for 35 hours a week or more.

If the person you care for got their decision letter for 1 of the following benefits in the last 3 months, you can backdate your Carer's Allowance to the start date in the letter:

Personal Independence Payment, Disability Living Allowance, Attendance Allowance, Constant Attendance Allowance or Armed Forces Independence Payment

Backdating longer than 3 months

If the benefit has been backdated for more than 3 months, you can use that date for your Carer's Allowance application. But only if you apply within 3 months of the date on the decision letter to award the benefit. This date will be on the decision letter of the person you care for.

2. The person you care for gets other benefits

What doesn't change

How much the person you care for gets from the following benefits won't change if you get Carer's Allowance:

Personal Independence Payment, Disability Living Allowance, Attendance Allowance, Constant Attendance Allowance or Armed Forces Independence Payment.

What can change

The person you care for might be severely disabled and gets extra money (a premium) from these benefits:

  • Jobseeker's Allowance income-based
  • Income Support
  • Employment and Support Allowance
  • Pension Credit
  • Housing Benefit

Once you get Carer's Allowance this extra money might stop. Talk to the helpline before applying.

If the person you care for pays reduced Council Tax, this could stop too. Talk to your local council about this before applying.

3. Your partner

How much Carer's Allowance you get depends on your household income. Your partner's benefits count towards this. Some of your partner's benefits might change if you get Carer's Allowance.

You should still claim because the total benefits you and your partner get will not change.

4. Breaks from caring

A break is any time you spent less than 35 hours a week caring for the other person.

For example when:

  • they were in respite care, hospital or on holiday without you
  • you were in hospital or on holiday without them

You might still get Carer's Allowance for these times.

Respite care and hospital breaks

You might get up to 12 weeks Carer's Allowance during these breaks.

During that time the person you care for must get Attendance Allowance, Disability Living Allowance (middle or highest rate) or Personal Independence Payment, Constant Attendance Allowance or Armed Forces Independence Payment.

Any breaks you reported in the last 6 months will count towards the 12 weeks.

Holidays

You might get a maximum of 4 weeks Carer's Allowance. Any holiday breaks you reported in the last 6 months will count towards the 4 weeks.

Money towards your State Pension during breaks

During times when you take a break from caring, money paid towards your State Pension is covered through Carer's Credit.

5. You're employed while getting Carer's Allowance

6. How you get paid

Carer's Allowance is paid every week or every 4 weeks, normally on a Monday. You can choose how often you want to be paid.

We'll write to you with the amount and the date of your first payment and with details of any arrears you are due.

Accounts you can use

We can pay money into these types of accounts:

  • bank account (yours, joint or someone else's)
  • Post Office card account (in your name only)
  • credit union account

If you're acting on behalf of the applicant, the account must be in your name.

Using someone else's account

The other person must agree that you can use their account. You must check the terms and conditions for the account to make sure they allow payments for another person.
